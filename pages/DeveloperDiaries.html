I included the primary scenes that would be needed, including the forest and parallax mechanism, what would happen when an animal was found, and how the user would wrap up the story and solve the mystery.
This helped me walk through our ideas in more logical steps and visualize how interactions might work. It also helped me identify problems we might face later that I didn’t think of during our initial planning stages.




I also created refined sketches for our concept art. We decided because some of our animals would be found in similar areas, each animal’s silhouette needed to really stand out. To try and solve the visual problem, we decided to refine our sketches drawing only the silhouettes first, and then adding line art to describe the features.

Thursday

We narrowed down our font pairings to a few different options. To make our final decisions, I used sketches we created for the wireframe and notepad and overlayed the text options on top. This helped us see the fonts in context. I also experimented with presenting the main text in different font sizes to mimic the style of the Reuters scrollytelling piece, Awash in Grief. We decided on having no dialogue boxes to simplify the interface and to make the project feel more like a storybook rather than a game.




As a team, we decided on using Volkorn for the main text and Providence Sans Pro - bold/regular for the notepad. A key consideration was what typefaces would be accessible (ex. Adobe fonts vs. free/Google fonts). We are able to use Adobe fonts for text that is apart of a larger image that we plan to export as pngs, however it would be better to use a Google font for reactive text that would coded directly on the site.


Saturday

I first refined Stef’s notepad sketch, using the same brush pack as Emily to keep the style consistent.


With the finished notepad asset, I created PNGs of each notepad page with animal facts. During this process, I realized that we had a LOT of text, and to keep the pages appropriate for our audience and highlight the image, we'd have to cut out a lot of it.


Emily and I also discussed the problem of resolution of the trees and bushes as you went deeper into the scene. We wanted to maintain the sketchy, watercolour textures, but to accommodate for desktop size, our Procreate canvas had to be very large. Because of the brush texture we were using, lines began to look pixelated when working on the new canvas dimensions.


We considered using illustrator to create each environment asset, but we weren't sure how to maintain the style we wanted. I knew of a feature called image trace, and used that to experiment with creating a vector version of our assets. I exported Emily's renders to Illustrator and used image trace to turn it into a vectorized image. I played with advanced settings, increasing the number of paths and corners to preserve as many of the colours and brush strokes as possible. We also played around with adding noise on a multiply layer and a flat color overlay on Illustrator to add back some of the grain/watercolour feeling. Although the new version looks slightly different, we figured out a workflow to create scalable vector assets are scalable that still have a hand-drawn feeling.

After looking at all of the completed assets, I had been feeling like the notepad asset didn’t fit as much. I decided to redo it to match the style of the other images — at this point I felt like I had a good grasp of how to design and colour it and felt confident enough to take it on. I simplified the top part of the notepad which was drawing a lot of attention (and frankly did not make sense now that I look at it — why would you have a spiral notepad with a tear away feature??) and changed the colour of the pen to match beanies bandana and create a more cohesive look. I also layered a paper texture on multiply overtop to achieve a scrapbook-like effect and applied a similar purple-blue gradient to unify the notepad with the background. After finishing the asset, I combined it with the new animal assets to recreate the completed notepad entries.



After this, I helped out with fixing the style of the buttons — Emily and I wanted them to also have a playful feeling — currently our buttons were styled in CSS but felt almost ad-like to us, especially when leaving the quiz. Emily helped design the new buttons and I was able to go into the code and replace the button with a png (this sounds easy but took me a good 7-8 hours to put in 4 buttons).
